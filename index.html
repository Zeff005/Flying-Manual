3.2 How to Pushback and Taxi

Pushback

  • At airports with active Ground control, always request pushback and check the correct tail direction for manual pushback.
  • At uncontrolled airports, use the map to ensure no oncoming traffic before pushing back.
  • Only initiate pushback when there is at least one gate's worth of space clear behind and in front of your aircraft.

Lights

  • Nav Lights should be set to "ON" when ground preparations are ongoing. Beacon lights must be set to "ON" before pushback has started.

Controls Check

  • After pushback, perform a full flight controls check, including yoke (ailerons, elevators) and rudder inputs to confirm control surface movement.
  • Ensure autobrakes are set to "MAX" and speedbrakes are armed before starting taxi.

Taxi

  • Make sure you announce on unicom to the runway you intend to taxi to, if there is no ATC. If there is, request ATC to runway.
🚗

3.3 Taxi Speeds and Regulations

Near Parking Gates

  • When near terminal parking, taxi speed must not exceed 10 knots ground speed.
  • Always anticipate oncoming traffic at busy airports and adjust taxi speed accordingly.
  • To maintain realism, avoid exceeding 40% N1 thrust while taxiing near terminals.

When Taxiing Towards Runway

  • Taxi towards the active runway at a maximum speed of 25 knots ground speed.
  • Racing or overtaking other aircraft to get ahead in line is strictly prohibited.
  • Keep the aircraft within taxiway boundaries and ensure the nose wheel remains aligned with the taxiway centerline.

While Making Turns

Maximum stipulated speeds for taxiing on airports:

  • Maximum 10 knots GS during 90-degree turns.
  • Maximum 15 knots GS during 45-degree turns.
  • Maximum 25 knots GS on straight taxiways.

Crossing an Active Runway During Taxi

  • When instructed to cross an active runway, switch strobe lights and landing lights ON before crossing the hold-short line.
  • Once the aircraft has fully cleared the runway (past the hold-short line on the other side), switch strobe and landing lights "OFF".

Exiting the Runway After Landing

  • Ensure that appropriate speeds are followed while performing high speed exits after landing. Exceeding 35 kts beyond the hold short line is strictly prohibited. Make sure the aircraft is fully clear of the hold short line before contacting ground control.

Other Regulations That Need to Be Followed

  • Always yield to aircraft approaching from your right while taxiing.
  • Follow ATC instructions at all times and maintain safe spacing.
  • Leave at least an "A320-sized gap" between your aircraft and the one ahead.

3.4 Takeoff and Climb

In this section, we will be giving a guideline on how to execute safe and secure takeoff. In Indian Virtual, we have a wide range of aircrafts to fly from, and each aircraft has a different weight range and takeoff speeds.

Calculating Take-off Speeds

You can use Simbrief's in-built tool to calculate takeoff speeds. Once you click this option, scroll down the menu that appears and click "Calculate". You'll come across the appropriate rotation speeds and flap speeds for your flight with this tool.

Pre-takeoff Checklists

  1. Ensure the Flight Plan (FPL) includes correct SIDs/STARs, has no duplicate or overlapping waypoints, and avoids sharp (90°) turns between close waypoints.
  2. Set flaps according to aircraft load and elevator trim to takeoff configuration.
  3. Set autobrake to MAX in case of a rejected takeoff.
  4. Turn strobe lights and landing lights ON before crossing the hold-short line.
  5. At uncontrolled airports, always check the map for incoming traffic and announce intentions on UNICOM before entering the runway.

Takeoff

  1. On "Line Up and Wait", align properly with the runway centerline.
  2. Rolling takeoffs are discouraged unless instructed for expedited departure.
  3. Before applying takeoff thrust, stabilize at 50% N1 for 2-3 seconds to prevent asymmetric power.
  4. Avoid using 100% N1; use tools like FlightSmart to calculate appropriate N1 based on weight and balance.
  5. Rotate smoothly, not exceeding 10-15° pitch or 3000 fpm. Positive climb: gear up.
  6. Climb at approximately 2500 fpm until 10,000 ft AGL at 250 knots IAS. Reduce thrust at 1500 ft AGL or as per SID to accelerate and retract flaps at proper speeds.
  7. Above 10,000 ft AGL, turn landing lights OFF, seatbelt signs OFF, and ensure a clean configuration.

Executing Crosswind Takeoffs

Taking-off in crosswinds is an art-form, and requires skills to execute properly. With the right amount of rudder and aileron deflection, you can safely take off from airports with high crosswind component.

You can follow the following guide from Infinite Flight to learn the nuances: Crosswind Landing - Airliner Tutorial

Climb Phase

  1. Use the #bot-commands channel on our Discord to access aircraft climb profiles with the aircraft stats command. (These are for reference and can be tweaked depending upon aircraft weight and airspace operations)
  2. When handed off to Departure or Center, simply "Check-In" unless further instructions are given.
  3. Continuously monitor Vertical Speed (VS) and Indicated Airspeed (IAS) until reaching cruise altitude.
  4. For long-haul flights, step-climbs are encouraged and are covered in the Cruise Section of this guide.

3.5 Cruise

Description

The cruise phase of flight begins once the aircraft has reached its initial cruising altitude and is flying level under stable conditions. This is typically the longest phase in a flight and is crucial for fuel efficiency and route adherence.

Guidelines for Cruising

  • Maintain assigned cruise altitude (based on flight plan and flight direction).
  • Monitor airspeed regularly. Adjust as needed to maintain fuel efficiency and aircraft performance.
  • Verify LNAV is active to ensure the aircraft continues to follow the flight plan.
  • Cross-check the ground speed and wind conditions occasionally, especially on long hauls. Stay within fuel range and aircraft limitations to avoid mid-flight issues.
  • If using AP+ (Autopilot Plus), ensure all AP+ conditions are met before exiting the app.

Standard Cruise Altitudes (based on direction)

  • Eastbound (000°-179°): Odd flight levels (FL310, FL330, FL350, etc.)
  • Westbound (180°-359°): Even flight levels (FL320, FL340, FL360, etc.)

3.6 Step Climb (For Long Haul Flights)

What is a Step Climb?

A step climb is a technique used during long-haul flights to gradually increase cruising altitude as fuel burns and the aircraft becomes lighter. This improves fuel efficiency and ensures optimal engine performance over time.

Why Use Step Climbs?

  • Reduces fuel consumption
  • Keeps aircraft within safe performance margins
  • Adapts to real-world operational practices for realism

How to Perform a Step Climb

  • Start at an initial cruise altitude based on aircraft weight and weather.
  • Monitor fuel burn and aircraft weight during cruise.
  • After a few hours (typically 2-4), when the aircraft is lighter, climb to the next optimal flight level (FL340 > FL360 > FL380, etc.).
  • Always climb in accordance with flight direction rules (odd/even levels).
  • Adjust Mach number and autopilot settings as required after each climb.

Best Practices

  • Step climbs are best used on flights 6+ hours in duration.
  • Use SimBrief or VA route planners to check optimal climb levels and estimated top-of-climb (TOC).

3.7 Arrival

Use of STAR (Standard Terminal Arrival Route)

A STAR is a predefined arrival route used to guide aircraft from cruise to approach. It provides lateral and vertical navigation down to the terminal area while avoiding restricted airspace and managing traffic flow.

Guidelines

  • Always select a STAR that matches your arrival runway and flight direction.
  • Load STARs from the Procedures tab in the flight plan menu in Infinite Flight.
  • Review altitude restrictions and speeds - follow them as closely as possible.
  • STARs often include waypoints that lead into the initial approach fix (IAF).
  • Choose a STAR based on active runway and ATC instructions, if available.

3.8 How to Request Approach to ATC

In this section, we will explain how to request ATC for approach in different situations. For pilots who are newly starting out in grade 3, this section can act as rough guide. However, we strictly encourage you to go over the official Infinite Flight flying guide before you set out your VA journey.

This Section is Divided into 3 Parts

  • 3.8.1 - Visual Approach
  • 3.8.2 - GPS Approach
  • 3.8.3 - ILS Approach

If You Approach into an Airport with Only an Approach Frequency

You tune into the approach Frequency. You DO NOT CHECK IN, rather directly state your approach request.

For example, If I tune into Mumbai Approach, I state, "Air India 001IN is 34 nautical miles north west of Mumbai at 12000ft requesting ILS approach at runway 27"

You will receive 2 possible responses from ATC:

  1. Continue as Filed, expect ILS approach at Runway 27.
  2. Expect Vectors for ILS Runway 27.

If you are asked to continue as filed:

  • Follow your flight plan into the airport and the ATC would hand you off to tower when needed.

If you are asked to expect vectors:

  • Be alert, since the ATC will assign you altitudes and headings until you approach the airport.

3.8.1 Visual Approach

Conditions for Visual Approach

  • You can be cleared to a Visual Approach if the pilot can visually locate the airport and the runway. For this to happen, Visual approaches are only possible and allowed if the visibility is more than 3sm (3 statute miles).
  • You will be positioned in a recognizable position such as downwind, base, or even straight in. The Approach Controller must then ask the aircraft to "report airport in sight"
  • Note that Controllers can still continue to issue vectors to the pilot after requesting the pilot to "report airport in sight"

3.8.2 GPS Approach

What are GPS approaches?

(GPS) Approach is a non-precision approach within IF but is not commonly used as functionality is currently limited. If however, pilots request this type of approach, Controllers may try to accommodate this when traffic permits.

When a pilot requests the GPS Approach and this is approved by the Controller (or if the GPS Approach is issued by the Controller).

Controllers will expect aircraft to continue on their FPL (or the new FPL if they had a different approach programmed previously). At an appropriate point (such as base), the Approach frequency will clear the aircraft for approach, but do not expect a final approach heading/altitude as the pilot is expected to follow their flight plan to the runway threshold.

You may only receive an Altitude/Heading command/vectors under 3 circumstances:

  • if a STAR does not lead aircraft onto the final approach
  • if it would not be expeditious to allow aircraft to fly their entire flight plan with CURRENT traffic levels
  • or if the aircraft has declared a missed approach

3.8.3 ILS Approach

What is an ILS approach?

ILS approach is perhaps the most commonly used method of approach in Infinite Flight and one of the simplest.

The ILS consists of two beams, the Localizer (LOC), providing lateral guidance (left/right) and the Glideslope (G/S) providing vertical guidance (up/down).

To intercept the G/S of an ILS, Controllers will issue an altitude that is below the G/S path.

Preparation before Approach:

  • Ensure your flight plan consists of the relevant ILS approach to the destination airport, and ensure the correct runway is selected.
  • Pilots need to be tuned into the appropriate ILS frequency of the arrival runway in the NAV1 tab.
  • Alternatively, you can also tap the airport, then head over to the "RWY" menu to find the runway altitudes and headings.
  • In case of an active approach frequency, you would be vectored into the ILS path by the controller.
  • Once cleared for ILS approach, tune into NAV1 mode from the GPS mode.
Note: Before commencing your base turn into the ILS path, ensure your speed is below 200 kts IAS, with the optimum speed being 180 kts IAS.

Intercepting the localizer:

  • Slow down to the appropriate approach speed. Make sure your flaps are extended gradually.
  • Continuously monitor the heading bug and the glideslope indicator (denoted by a green diamond). Once the glideslope indicator is in range and you're aligned with the runway, arm "APPR" if it's available. Else, manually follow this path.
  • Once glideslope has been captured, make minimal changes to speed and flaps configuration. While not always necessary, it can sometimes be helpful to lower the landing gear before APPR mode is armed. This ensures a stabilized approach onto the runway.
  • Disconnect the autopilot (preferably before 200 ft AGL) and hand-fly into the runway while managing speed as well as pitch. Ensure the FPV (flight path vector) is directly aimed at the runway centerline.

3.8.4 Stabilized Approach Criteria

The following criteria should be satisfied when you're set up for landing.

Correct Aircraft Configuration

  • All aircraft must be fully configured for landing no later than 800 feet AGL. This includes landing gear down, flaps set to final landing position, spoilers armed, and landing checklist completed. 800 feet AGL is the absolute last point by which the aircraft must be in its final landing configuration.
  • Below 800 feet AGL, no further changes to flaps or gear are permitted. If the pilot intends to land in Config 3, that setting must be selected and stabilized prior to 800 feet AGL. Any flap selection or gear extension below 800 ft AGL is considered an unstabilized approach and must result in a go-around.
  • Autobrake setting is not restricted by altitude. It must be selected during the descent phase, but pilots may adjust or change it at any time during the approach, based on runway conditions, performance needs, or personal preference.

Proper Speed Management

  1. Flaps Approach Speed -- ~10 NM / 2000 ft AGL
  2. Final Approach Speed (Vref - 5 +15 kts) -- Achieved by 1000 feet AGL

SimBrief's in-built tool would also help you with landing speeds calculations.

Your desired approach speeds as well as flap settings are automatically calculated using this tool.

4.1 Guide to PIREPs

In Indian Virtual, PIREPs are a way to keep a track and to check whether a pilot has really flown the route they have filed and see its legitimacy.

PIREPs are filed by pilots and we at Indian Virtual also allow IFATC PIREPs and rules are different for each.

In the subtabs below, we will show how it is to file a PIREP in Indian Virtual in different scenarios!

✏️

4.1.1 Guide to PIREPs for Pilots

Important: You must file a PIREP after a flight is complete. Do not file before the flight. Additionally, PIREPS need to be filed by 48 hours after the completion of the flight. If filed after that, they would be rejected without notice.

If you're asked to refile a particular PIREP, you have a 72 hour block to file it again, and if filed later, they would be rejected without any further explanation.

There are 2 ways you can file a PIREP as a pilot in INVA

  1. Manual PIREP filing
  2. Filing a PIREP by selecting a route

Manual PIREP Filling

  1. Go to the PIREP submission page.
  2. Manually add all the flight information - Flight Hours, DEP and Arrival ICAO, Flight Number of the flight that is available in the database. Not your VA callsign number.
  3. Make sure to add the proper operator. In this case I flew Air India, so my operator must be Indian Virtual.
  4. If you are applicable to add a multiplier, make sure to select the right multiplier (For example, CEO Routes 1.5x)
  5. After selecting Multiplier from the multiplier tab, make sure to write in the comments of the reason/the multiplier you select. It is because we have too many 1.5x multipliers, we can only see the 1.5x selected but not the exact multiplier that you selected.

PIREP Filing from Routes Section

  1. Click on the Routes section in the Crew Center
  2. Enter the route you have flown/the flight number. For example I have flown VIDP-EHAM (AI155), I enter the Flight Number or the ICAO - ICAO or both and then press search.
  3. Press FILE PIREP button which is present on the corresponding flight.
  4. It should redirect to this screen with the Flight Number/ICAO-ICAO filed already. You must now file the flight hours that you accumulated from flying and the aircraft used on this route.

CRITERIA OF THE MULTIPLIERS

1. IFATC PIREP - ON SCHEDULE INDIAN AIRPORT (1.0x)

You may use this multiplier, the airport you control is on the schedule for that particular day on the IFATC Schedule.

If I control VABB on the 15th, then I may select the ON SCHEDULE INDIAN AIRPORT (1.0x) Multiplier as the Indian airport is on schedule.

2. IFATC PIREP - INVA EVENT NON INDIAN AIRPORT

You may use this multiplier, if you control an event/group flight hosted by executive members in Indian Virtual for an airport that is a NON Indian Airport. For example, Indian Virtual departs out of KJFK for VABB, you control KJFK GTS and provide your ATC service for INVA pilots, you are applicable for this multiplier.

3. IFATC PIREP - NON SCHEDULE INDIAN AIRPORT

You may use this multiplier, if you open any Indian Airport on a day that airport isn't featured in the ATC Schedule.

For example, on June 12th, I open VABB GTS for 1h, I must use this multiplier "NON SCHEDULE INDIAN AIRPORT" As the airports featured for this day, isn't VABB.

4. IFATC PIREP - SCHEDULE AIRPORT

You may use this multiplier if you open an airport in the schedule. I open LOWW GTS on 12th June, I must use this multiplier.

YOU must also use this multiplier if you open any airport that isn't on the schedule (Non Indian Airport). For example, after I open KLAX GTS on the 12th June, I must use this multiplier while filing my PIREP.

5. IFATC PIREP - INVA EVENT INDIAN AIRPORT

You may use this multiplier if you open an Indian Airport for Indian Virtual events/group flights which are hosted by the staff team.

5.0 AP+ Rules

Rules within Indian Virtual

With the release of the AP+ update (25.1), pilot activity is expected to increase significantly. To maintain fairness and integrity within Indian Virtual (INVA), the following rules apply:

  • Single INVA Flight Rule
    Only one INVA flight may be active at a time, either via AP+ or on the Expert Server. A second flight is allowed only for leisure, not for logging with INVA.
  • No Double PIREPs
    Filing two flights at the same time for INVA (e.g., one on AP+ and one active) is strictly prohibited.
  • No Simultaneous Flying for Other VAs
    You cannot fly for INVA on one server (e.g., AP+) and simultaneously fly for another VA on another server. Only one VA flight at a time is permitted.

These rules ensure fair play and uphold the standards of Indian Virtual operations.

Note: Always Mention AP+ while filing Career Mode Pireps if you have flown using it.

What is AP+?

AutoPilot+ (AP+) is a feature in Infinite Flight that allows a pilot's aircraft to continue flying on autopilot after exiting the app during cruise. This ensures that long-haul flights can still be completed without keeping the device running.

AP+ is especially useful for Virtual Airline pilots completing long routes, allowing more flexibility while maintaining professionalism and realism.

When Does AP+ Activate?

AP+ activates automatically when the following conditions are met:

Requirement Description
Cruise Altitude Jets at or above FL280 (28,000 ft), Props at or above 7,000 ft
Level Flight The aircraft must be stable at cruising altitude (no climb/descent)
Autopilot + LNAV Enabled Autopilot with LNAV (lateral navigation) must be active
Filed Flight Plan A complete route must be filed and being followed
More Than 30 Min Remaining ETE (Estimated Time En Route) must show 30+ minutes left in cruise
On-Track Aircraft must be properly aligned with the flight plan route

Once these conditions are met, exiting the flight from the main menu will automatically activate AP+.

How Does AP+ Work?

  • The aircraft continues flying in the background, maintaining:
    • Current altitude
    • Track (LNAV route)
    • Ground speed
  • It uses a snapshot of current flight conditions but does NOT adjust for changing winds or live weather.
  • Position is saved every 5 minutes, so the flight resumes close to where you left.

AP+ Auto Pausing Before Arrival

  • AP+ will pause the flight automatically ~30 minutes before Top of Descent.
  • You'll be removed from the live server and can resume the flight within 48 hours.

How to Resume a Flight

  1. Open Infinite Flight
  2. Tap "Continue Flight" on the main menu
  3. You'll rejoin the flight exactly where it paused
  4. Manually resume control for descent, approach, and landing
  5. ATC communication resumes if you're in an active airspace

9.0 Recruitment Checklist/Guidelines (For Recruits only)

INVA conducts 2 practical tests before a new pilot is selected. These practical tests ensure that recruits have a decent level of competency to fly on the expert server. The two tests are:

  • Pattern work test
  • Flying between hubs of the day

Recruits are expected to read the official Infinite Flight flying manual before attempting tests and in case of any doubts, they are free to ask doubts within the recruitment ticket channel itself.

Note: Expect your test results within 24-48 hours after taking the tests. Avoid pinging the Flight Instructors to speed up this process.

Some General Tips to Ace the Test:

  • Have a very good idea of pattern work within Infinite Flight. Use the official guide to learn terms such as upwind, downwind, base and final. Ensure you adhere to pattern altitudes and speeds as well. Additionally, you can find detailed guides on YouTube or IFC.
  • Ensure that your takeoffs are well stabilized and do not exceed 15 degrees pitch. Use SimBrief's own takeoff and landing calculations to ensure realistic speeds. Refer to section 3.4 for further information.
  • A similar approach is to be followed while executing landings, ensuring that your approach speeds are not too high or low and is within acceptable range. Refer to section 3.8.4 to learn what a "stabilized" approach criteria is.
  • Ensure your downwind and base legs are not too wide/tight that makes your aircraft overshoot the approach path.
  • Have a fair idea of ILS approaches and Visual approaches before attempting the test.
  • Ensure you know how to use the unicom frequency as our flight instructors would closely monitor your ATC competency. Incorrect call-outs on the unicom frequency can be a cause of rejection in the recruitment test.
Note: If you are an IFATC member, you are automatically exempted from taking both the tests.
